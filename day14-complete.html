üìñ Growing Together

Chapter 1: The Empty Lot

Twelve-year-old Rosa Delgado walked past the empty lot on Maple Street every day on her way to Riverside Middle School. The lot had been vacant for as long as she could remember‚Äîa rectangular patch of weedy grass surrounded by a chain-link fence. Old fast-food containers and plastic bags caught in the fence fluttered in the wind, and the whole area looked forgotten and neglected.

One morning in early spring, Rosa noticed something different. Mrs. Chen, an elderly woman who lived in the house next to the lot, was standing at the fence with a clipboard in her hands. She was measuring something and making notes, occasionally looking up at the sky as if considering how much sunlight the area received.

"Good morning, Mrs. Chen," Rosa called out as she approached. "What are you doing?"

Mrs. Chen looked up with a warm smile. "Hello, Rosa. I'm thinking about this empty space. You know, in my home country of Taiwan, we had beautiful community gardens where neighbors grew vegetables together. I've been wondering if we could create something like that here."

Rosa looked at the lot with new eyes. Instead of seeing weeds and trash, she began to imagine rows of vegetables, colorful flowers, and maybe even a small seating area where people could gather. "That sounds amazing! But how would something like that work? Who owns this land?"

Mrs. Chen explained that she had been researching the property. The city owned the lot, and there was actually a program that allowed communities to apply for permits to create gardens on unused municipal land. "The challenge," she said, "is that we need to show there's genuine community interest and that we have people willing to maintain the garden year-round."

Rosa felt excitement building inside her. "I think lots of people would be interested! My family loves gardening, and my mom always complains about how expensive fresh vegetables are at the store. Plus, my science teacher, Mr. Rodriguez, is always talking about how important it is for communities to have green spaces."

Chapter 2: Building Support

That evening, Rosa talked to her parents about Mrs. Chen's idea. Her mother, Maria, was immediately enthusiastic. "A community garden would be wonderful! We could grow the vegetables we use for cooking‚Äîtomatoes, peppers, cilantro, onions. And it would be a great way to meet our neighbors better."

Rosa's father, Carlos, was more cautious. "It sounds like a big project, mija. Who's going to coordinate everything? How will we decide what to plant? What about watering and maintaining the garden during the summer when some families go on vacation?"

These were good questions, and Rosa realized that successful community projects required careful planning and organization. She decided to approach her science teacher, Mr. Rodriguez, for advice.

"Community gardens are excellent examples of sustainable urban agriculture," Mr. Rodriguez told her the next day. "They provide fresh food, create green spaces, and bring people together. But you're right that they require good organization. Have you thought about forming a planning committee?"

Rosa hadn't considered that, but it made sense. Mr. Rodriguez suggested that she and Mrs. Chen organize a community meeting to gauge interest and begin forming a leadership group. "You'll need people with different skills‚Äîsomeone who knows about gardening, someone who can handle the paperwork and permits, someone who can manage communications with neighbors."

Over the next two weeks, Rosa and Mrs. Chen worked together to spread the word about their idea. They created flyers in both English and Spanish, posted them in local shops and community centers, and went door-to-door talking to neighbors. Rosa was surprised by how many people were interested. Mr. Jackson, a retired engineer who lived two blocks away, offered to help with the design and layout. Ms. Patel, who managed the local library, volunteered to research grant opportunities for funding.

The response was so positive that Rosa began to believe their dream might actually become reality.

Chapter 3: Overcoming Challenges

The first community meeting was held at the library on a Thursday evening in April. Rosa was nervous as she looked around the room at nearly thirty neighbors who had come to hear about the garden project. Mrs. Chen stood up to address the group.

"Thank you all for coming," she began. "As many of you know, Rosa and I have been exploring the possibility of creating a community garden in the empty lot on Maple Street. We believe this project could bring fresh food, natural beauty, and stronger connections to our neighborhood."

The response was mostly positive, but several people raised important concerns. Mrs. Williams, who lived directly across from the lot, worried about increased traffic and noise. "Will people be working in the garden at all hours? What about parking when gardeners come to tend their plots?"

Mr. Thompson, a longtime resident, questioned whether the project would be sustainable. "I've seen community initiatives start with great enthusiasm and then fall apart when the work gets hard. What happens when people lose interest or move away?"

Rosa realized these were valid concerns that needed to be addressed. She stood up, her heart beating fast. "These are really important questions. Maybe we could create guidelines about garden hours‚Äîlike only from sunrise to sunset, and no power tools after 7 PM. And for sustainability, we could require people to commit to at least one year of participation before getting a plot."

Mr. Rodriguez, who had come to support Rosa, added his thoughts. "Successful community gardens often have backup systems. They might partner with local schools or organizations that can help maintain the garden during slow periods. They also usually have small membership fees that go toward shared tools and infrastructure."

By the end of the meeting, the group had decided to form a planning committee with eight members representing different aspects of the project: garden design, permits and legal issues, community outreach, funding, maintenance scheduling, conflict resolution, partnerships with local organizations, and youth involvement.

Rosa was elected to represent youth involvement, and she felt proud to be trusted with such an important role.

Chapter 4: Dreams Take Root

Over the next four months, the planning committee met every two weeks to work through the details of creating their community garden. Rosa learned that successful community projects require attention to many details she had never considered.

The permit process was more complex than anyone had expected. The city required soil testing to ensure the land was safe for growing food, a detailed plan showing how the garden would be organized, proof of insurance coverage, and signatures from 75% of the neighbors within a two-block radius.

Ms. Patel's research into funding opportunities paid off when she discovered that the city offered grants of up to $5,000 for community green space projects. However, the application required a detailed budget, a timeline for implementation, and evidence of community support.

Rosa found her role as youth coordinator both challenging and rewarding. She organized a group of twelve students from her school who were interested in participating in the garden. They met after school once a week to learn about gardening techniques, sustainable agriculture, and project management.

The student group decided to focus on creating an educational component for the garden. They designed a section that would demonstrate different types of plants‚Äîvegetables, herbs, flowers that attract beneficial insects, and native plants that support local wildlife. They also planned to create informational signs explaining sustainable gardening practices.

By late summer, all the paperwork was approved, the grant funding was secured, and the soil testing showed that the land was suitable for growing food. The official groundbreaking ceremony was scheduled for the first Saturday in September.

On that sunny September morning, Rosa stood at the edge of the lot‚Äîno longer empty, but filled with neighbors carrying shovels, wheelbarrows, and plants. Mrs. Chen walked over to her with a small tomato plant.

"This is a cutting from the tomato plant my grandmother brought from Taiwan fifty years ago," Mrs. Chen said, handing Rosa the plant. "I think it's fitting that you should plant the first seedling in our new garden."

As Rosa carefully placed the plant in the rich, dark soil, she realized that they had done much more than create a garden. They had created a community‚Äîa group of people who had learned to work together, solve problems, and turn a shared dream into reality.

Looking around at her neighbors working side by side, Rosa understood that the most important thing growing in their garden wasn't vegetables or flowers. It was the connections between people who had discovered they could accomplish amazing things when they worked together.

Answer Key with Explanations

1. A) There is an empty, neglected lot that could be better used

STAAR Skill: Identifying main problem/conflict. The story describes the lot as "vacant," "weedy," and "forgotten and neglected," which Rosa and Mrs. Chen want to transform.

2. C) "Rosa looked at the lot with new eyes"

STAAR Skill: Analyzing character development. This sentence shows Rosa's perspective changing from seeing weeds and trash to imagining possibilities.

3. D) Real, sincere support from many community members

STAAR Skill: Determining word meaning from context. The word "genuine" means real or sincere, and the context shows it means actual committed support.

4. C) He is thoughtful and considers practical challenges

STAAR Skill: Making inferences about character. Carlos asks important questions about coordination, decision-making, and maintenance, showing careful thinking.

5. A) To ensure the project has good organization and different skills

STAAR Skill: Understanding cause and effect. Mr. Rodriguez explains that successful gardens need "good organization" and "people with different skills."

6. C) "Nearly thirty neighbors had come to hear about the garden project"

STAAR Skill: Using text evidence to support ideas. The large turnout at the meeting demonstrates widespread community interest.

7. C) To include all community members regardless of their primary language

STAAR Skill: Understanding author's purpose. Creating bilingual flyers shows inclusivity and ensures all community members can participate.

8. D) She listens to concerns and offers thoughtful solutions

STAAR Skill: Analyzing character actions. Rosa acknowledges concerns as "valid" and "important" and offers specific solutions like garden hours and commitment requirements.

Write About Community Projects

Choose one of these prompts and write a 200-300 word persuasive text:

Option 1: Propose a Community Project

Write a proposal for a community project in your neighborhood (like a playground, recycling program, or reading corner). Explain why it's needed and how it would benefit the community.

Option 2: Letter to City Council

Write a letter to your city council supporting community gardens in your area. Use evidence from the story and other sources to make your argument.

Option 3: Convince Your Neighbors

Write a persuasive flyer encouraging neighbors to join a community project. Address potential concerns and highlight the benefits of participation.

Writing Tips:

  • State your main argument clearly
  • Use specific examples and evidence
  • Address potential concerns or objections
  • Use persuasive language and transitions
  • End with a strong call to action
